Someone in your city recently searched for your services on their phone. They didn't browse multiple pages, compare websites, or read blog posts. Instead, they saw three to five business names at the top of Google — above everything else — each marked with a blue badge, a star rating, and a phone number. Then they called one of them.

These service-based businesses aren't just ranking well. They have moved to the top of Google using Google Local Service Ads to attract real leads from actual customers — before anyone else even gets a chance to compete.

What Local Service Ads Actually Are

Google Local Service Ads (LSAs) appear above all other Google results — including the map, paid search ads, and organic listings. They are the first thing users see when searching for services like a plumber, HVAC technician, roofer, lawyer, or cleaning service, among many other categories supported by Google.

Every Other Ad Type
Pay Per Click
You pay every time someone clicks your ad — whether they're a real customer, a competitor, or just curious. Budget evaporates fast regardless of whether anyone actually contacts you.
Local Service Ads
Pay Per Lead
You pay only for a real phone call or message from a genuine person in your service area seeking your specific service. If nobody contacts you, you pay nothing.

The Google Verified badge — a blue checkmark next to your business name — shows that Google has verified your background, licenses, and insurance. This badge builds trust before any words are spoken and can appear dynamically on your Google Business Profile as well, offering additional value across Google's surfaces.

Your ranking isn't solely based on how much you spend. Google uses an auction-style system where profile quality and performance — reviews, responsiveness, and conversion data — play a major role alongside your bid. A small local business with a strong profile, consistent reviews, and excellent lead-handling can often outrank a larger competitor with a bigger budget and a neglected listing.

Part One: The Foundation You Cannot Skip

Each LSA profile is connected to a Google Business Profile. If your Google Business Profile is disorganized, your LSAs won't perform well — regardless of how much you optimize everything else. Before touching LSA setup, assess these five areas:

NAP Consistency
Your business name, address, and phone number must match exactly across all platforms — Google Business Profile, website, Yelp, BBB, Facebook, and every directory listing. Even a single incorrect digit or different abbreviation can harm your trustworthiness score.
Verification Status
Unverified Google Business Profiles are not eligible for LSAs. Claim and verify your listing if you haven't already done so.
Duplicate Listings
Listings created by third parties or outdated profiles from previous locations can damage your credibility with Google. Locate these duplicates and merge or remove them.
Real Photos
Profiles with genuine photos outperform those without. Use real images — your truck, your team, completed work, storefront. Authentic photos send legitimate business signals to Google's algorithm.
Accurate Hours
Your listed hours must match your actual operating hours. Incorrect hours lower your responsiveness score, which directly affects your LSA ranking.

The Google Verified Process

To run LSAs, you must complete Google's screening and verification process. It cannot be rushed, but preparation makes it faster. Here is what you will need:

  • Your current, valid business license — must match your business name exactly as it appears on your Google Business Profile
  • A certificate of general liability insurance with coverage that meets Google's minimums for your category
  • Workers' compensation documentation, if you have employees
  • Consent to background checks for you and, in some categories, your field staff

The most common reason applications stall: The name on the insurance certificate doesn't exactly match the name on the Google Business Profile. Check this before you submit. It may seem minor, but it can cause delays of weeks. Match every word and punctuation mark.

Important Update — October 20, 2025

Google combined its previous badges (Google Guaranteed, Google Screened, License Verified) into a single Google Verified badge and discontinued the associated Money Back Guarantee program. All existing LSA profiles transitioned to the new unified badge automatically.

Once verified, the Google Verified badge appears on your profile. This isn't just for looks — it signals to searchers that you've passed Google's license, insurance, and background screening. Industry data consistently shows that this type of verification significantly increases call rates. It is a trust indicator that no amount of advertising spend can replicate.

Part Two: The Ranking Algorithm

Google has never released a comprehensive official LSA ranking guide, but key factors are outlined in their Help Center. The following is based on official Google sources, industry data, direct testing, and pattern recognition across different markets and service categories.

1
Reviews — Quality, Recency, and Velocity
Your star rating and reviews are pulled directly from your Google Business Profile. Google evaluates your rating, how recently reviews were posted, and how consistently you are receiving new ones. A 4.5 star baseline is effectively required in competitive markets.
2
Responsiveness — Reply Rate and Speed
Google tracks how quickly you respond to leads from LSAs. Your response rate and average reply time appear on your dashboard and directly influence your ranking. Slow response or no response hurts your position.
3
Bid Amount
Your bid contributes to ranking but does not override profile quality signals. Spending more with a weak profile will not overcome a competitor with a well-managed lower-bid profile.
4
Proximity to the Searcher
Google factors in the distance between your listed service area and where the searcher is located. A focused, accurate service area outperforms a sprawling, unfocused one in conversion signals.
5
Business Hours and Profile Completeness
Whether you are currently open when the search happens, and how completely your profile is filled out, both factor into placement. An incomplete profile signals low investment to Google's algorithm.

Reviews: The Biggest Lever You Have

Your star rating and reviews in LSAs are pulled directly from your Google Business Profile. There are no longer separate native LSA reviews — all reviews are managed through your GBP and influence both your profile authority and LSA visibility.

What the Algorithm Values

  • Star Rating. A 4.5 or higher is effectively the baseline in competitive markets. Below that, you're climbing a steep hill.
  • Recency. A business with 300 reviews and no new posts in four months will rank lower than one with 60 reviews and five posted last week. Old reviews lose relevance regardless of how many there are.
  • Velocity. Consistently gaining reviews outperforms sporadic spikes. Five reviews each month, month after month, beats 60 reviews in January and none afterward.
50
Reviews before your profile is considered competitive in most local markets
100+
Reviews to be truly dominant — quantity is strong social proof in Google's algorithm
4.5★
Minimum star rating to be competitive. Below this you are working against the algorithm

The proven method: Send a text message with a direct link to your Google review page right after the customer shows satisfaction. Text messages have significantly higher open rates than emails. Make this an official step in your workflow — train your technicians or front desk staff to do it consistently and track the results. Review acquisition is not just a marketing task. It is an operational one.

Responsiveness: The Hidden Factor That Affects Your Rankings

Google tracks how quickly you respond to leads from LSAs. Your response rate and average reply time appear on your dashboard and directly influence your ranking. This makes sense from Google's perspective: they are sending searchers your way. If you don't reply, the searcher has a negative experience, and Google responds by lowering the rank of unresponsive businesses.

  • Respond within five minutes during business hours. After hours, respond at the start of the next business day.
  • Set up call forwarding if you are a solo operator or small team so LSA calls reach a real person, not voicemail.
  • Consider a professional answering service for after-hours coverage if your volume justifies it.
  • Dispute leads you can't service rather than leaving them unanswered. Letting unanswered leads sit can hurt your responsiveness score even if there is a valid reason not to reply.

Service Area: Be Strategic, Not Greedy

When setting up your LSA profile, you'll specify the cities and zip codes you serve. Most business owners choose as many as possible — and this is almost always a mistake.

Google's algorithm gauges your performance within your defined service area. If you're appearing in zip codes where you rarely win business, that conversion data lowers your overall score. Focus on the core geography where you consistently perform work and close jobs. Expand from there as your data supports it. A focused, high-performing service area will rank higher than a larger, less targeted one.

The Service Area Principle

A contractor serving Goshen and Elkhart with a tight, well-performing service area will outrank a competitor claiming to serve all of Northern Indiana but only winning jobs in three zip codes. Honesty in service area definition is not a limitation — it is a ranking advantage.

Budget: LSA Works Differently Than Traditional Advertising

LSA budgets work differently from paid search. You set a weekly cap, and Google spreads your spending over the week to generate leads within that limit.

The common mistake is setting a budget so low that the ads stop showing by the second week of the month. When your budget runs out, you disappear from the top of Google for the rest of the billing period — regardless of how strong your profile is. Set a budget that keeps you visible for the entire month. During peak season, increase it proactively. Reactive budget increases after you've already gone dark cost you leads you may never recover.

Track your cost per lead, your close rate, and your average job value. These numbers tell you exactly what your LSA budget is worth and how much you should be willing to spend to stay visible.

Part Three: Profile Optimization Most People Ignore

Your Business Description

Use your limited characters to clearly state three things: what you do, where you do it, and why you're the trustworthy choice. Mention your city and region explicitly. Include a specific trust signal — how long you've been in business, your license number, or your Google Verified status. Be direct. Searchers aren't reading for entertainment. They have a problem and need someone to solve it.

Job Types

Your profile lets you select specific job types within your service category. List only the job types you actually perform — each one signals to Google that you're qualified to appear for that search query. Avoid listing job types you don't do. Google tracks how well your listed services match the leads you receive. Mismatches harm your profile's performance score.

Photos

Use real photos only. Show your team, vehicles, work in progress, completed projects, and your office or storefront. Profiles with authentic, specific photos perform better in click and call rates than those with stock images. When customers are inviting someone into their home, visual trust matters at every stage of the decision.

Business Hours

List the hours when you're actually reachable by phone. If your hours indicate you're open but calls go to voicemail, your responsiveness score drops. Accuracy here protects your ranking — list the hours you can genuinely back up with a live answer.

Part Four: Managing Your Leads Like a Pro

Dispute Leads That Don't Qualify

Google charges you for leads, but not all leads are legitimate charges. You can and should dispute leads that fall into these categories:

✕
The caller needed a service you don't offer
✕
The caller was outside your service area
✕
Spam call, robocall, or supplier sales call
✕
Duplicate lead for a job already in progress
✕
No actual service request — wrong number or immediate hangup
✕
Call from a job seeker or vendor, not a customer

Review your leads regularly and dispute ineligible ones promptly. Contractors and service businesses that actively manage disputes recover a significant portion of their spending over time. Google credits approved disputes. It is your money — claim it.

Read Your Dashboard Data

Your LSA dashboard shows lead volume, cost per lead, booked jobs, response rate, and a breakdown of leads by job type. Most business owners glance at this occasionally but rarely act on it. The operators who excel in their local markets review this data monthly, treating it as a business scorecard: which job types generate the most leads? Which generates leads that don't convert? Where is cost per lead rising? Where is response rate declining? This data shows you where to invest more and where to cut back.

Part Five: The Window That Is Open Right Now

LSA rankings are shaped by past performance. Google favors businesses with a steady history of responsiveness, good reviews, and dependable results. This history builds up over time, making it increasingly difficult for new competitors to close the gap.

In most local markets, the LSA landscape is still in flux. Businesses with strong reputations and loyal clients often have weak LSA profiles, no review system, and slow lead response. They remain invisible at the top of Google for key searches — even as strong local brands offline.

The pattern repeats across all local service markets: Early adopters gain a lasting edge. The businesses that build strong LSA profiles now, while competitors are still ignoring the channel, will be building ranking history that new entrants will have to spend significantly more to compete with. In some markets that window is already closing. In others, it is still wide open. The question is which one your market is.

Your Action List for This Week

1
Audit Your Google Business Profile
Check NAP consistency, verified status, accurate hours, and real photos. Fix anything that is wrong before moving forward. This is the foundation everything else builds on.
2
Start or Finish Your Google Verified Process
Have your license and insurance documents ready before you begin. Confirm your business name matches exactly across every document. A single mismatch delays the process by weeks.
3
Set Up a Text-Based Review Request System
Send a review request text with a direct link to your GBP review page after every completed job, starting today. Make this an official operational step — not an occasional marketing task.
4
Define Your Service Area Honestly
Set your service area based on where you actually work and win business — not where you wish you worked. Expand from a strong core rather than starting wide and thin.
5
Set a Budget That Keeps You Visible All Month
Calculate a weekly cap that sustains visibility through the full month with room to increase during peak season. Going dark is more expensive than staying visible.
6
Create a Lead Response Plan
Decide who answers LSA calls, what happens after hours, and how to escalate when the primary contact is unavailable. Five minutes is the target response time during business hours.
7
Review and Dispute Your Lead History
Go through your existing lead history and dispute any charges that don't meet Google's criteria. Recover overpaid spend through Google's dispute and credit process.
8
Open Your Dashboard Once a Month
Review lead volume, cost per lead, booked jobs, response rate, and job type breakdown. Treat it as a business scorecard. The data tells you where to invest more and where to cut back.
